# When are supercapacitors practically feasible in electric vehicles?

Abstract 

While the hybrid energy storage system (HESS) can theoretically mitigate battery degradation in electric vehicles, its practical implementation remains highly limited. To delineate the specific scenarios and application boundaries where su-percapacitors remain feasible, this study proposes a multi-dimensional techno-economic feasibility evaluation framework. First, a cross-vehicle sizing method based on dynamic programming is established to quantify physical mass-volume pack-aging constraints and identify feasible supercapacitor candidates across di ff erent vehicle types. Building upon the optimal sizing parameters derived from the battery aging Pareto front, an expert-guided deep reinforcement learning energy man-agement strategy is integrated to yield near-optimal online performance, ensuring a fair life-cycle economic assessment. Finally, a comprehensive feasibility matrix is constructed to systematically evaluate mass, volume, battery lifespan, addi-tional supercapacitor costs, total cost of ownership, future energy storage prices, and the influence of emerging solid-state batteries. Results reveal that city buses remain the most promising vehicle type for HESS due to minimal additional costs and su ffi cient packaging space. Current mass-volume penalties and limited economic benefits hinder HESS applica-tion in passenger vehicles and heavy-duty trucks, respectively. This situation may only improve if supercapacitor prices drop significantly in the future. Beyond vehicle types, the HESS feasibility is governed by load-frequency characteris-tics. Furthermore, looking toward the 2030 + solid-state battery era, we highlight that integrating increasingly a ff ordable supercapacitors can provide substantial asset protection leverage. 

Keywords: Hybrid energy storage system; Optimal sizing; Optimal energy management; Mass and Volume; Solid-state battery; Feasibility evaluation. 

1. Introduction 

1.1. Background and motivation 

Battery degradation induced by high-frequency and high-amplitude dynamic loads remains a critical challenge in electric vehicles. The hybrid energy storage system (HESS) that couples a high-energy-density battery pack with a high-power-density supercapacitor (SC) pack has been widely recognized as a theoretically e ff ective solution [1]. By absorbing transient peak currents [2], the superca-pacitor e ff ectively protects the battery from severe electro-chemical stress, thereby extending its lifespan and improv-ing the overall vehicle economy [3]. Despite extensive studies demonstrating the theoretical benefits for the past decade [4], the commercial adoption of HESS in practical electric vehicles remains highly lim-ited, indicating that the theoretical lifespan extension does not automatically translate into engineering implementa-tion. The resistance to the practical application primarily originates from three aspects: (1) optimal SC sizing, (2) advanced onboard energy management strategy, and (3) overall techno-economic feasibility. 

1.2. Literature review and research gap 

In terms of sizing, existing research has focused on de-termining the most cost-favorable supercapacitor capaci-ties by balancing electrical parameters and initial invest-ment costs. To achieve this, dynamic programming (DP) [5] is adopted for the global optimal energy management of electric buses, enabling the traversal of di ff erent super-capacitor combinations to identify the optimal candidate on the Pareto frontier of HESS life cost. Genetic algo-rithm is employed [6] to determine the optimal HESS siz-ing by concurrently minimizing the system weight, manu-facturing cost, and battery capacity degradation. A univer-sal double-layer HESS sizing optimization method is pro-posed for diverse topological configurations [7]. Further-more, Wang et al. [8] simultaneously optimized the HESS sizing and energy management with the dual objectives of minimizing supercapacitor costs and battery degradation. Huang et al. [9] considered the mass and the resulting power load increase from additional supercapacitor packs, noting that the cost Pareto frontier is not monotonic. For other vehicle types, such as light commercial vehicles [10], HESS sizing can be coupled with thermal management to minimize life-cycle costs under extreme temperatures. For electric racing cars, a hierarchical sizing method that inte-

grates lateral dynamics is proposed to minimize race time [11]. Beyond sizing, advanced onboard energy management strategies are critical for HESS to extend battery life or re-duce operation costs. The common objective is to yield results close to o ffl ine DP. A model predictive control (MPC)-based energy management strategy is proposed with a single tuning parameter balancing SC voltage regu-lation and battery current smoothing [12]. A vehicle-cloud energy management strategy is proposed [13], leveraging DP and MPC to optimize real-time power allocation for HESS in mining trucks. Multi-horizon MPC using longer velocity prediction (hundreds of seconds) can further ex-tend battery life and reduce operation cost [14] and even applied to integrated battery thermal and energy manage-ment [15]. Reinforcement learning [16], incentive deep reinforcement learning (DRL) [17], and DRL with genera-tive adversarial imitation learning [18] are also promising onboard energy management strategies for battery life op-timization in HESS. Recently, online updating for DRL-based energy management strategy [19] also enhances the continual improvement capability. Building upon sizing and energy management, various studies evaluated the techno-economic feasibility of HESS from the perspectives of experimental implementation and life-cycle cost analysis. To evaluate economic feasibility, a comprehensive sensitivity analysis across various vehi-cle, system, and component parameters is conducted [20] to quantify their impacts on the HESS life-cycle financial costs. An economic analysis of passenger vehicles with HESS under vehicle-following scenarios [21] shows that the battery degradation cost accounts for 2 /3 of the total operation cost. Veneri et al. [22] constructed a 1:1 scale experimental test bench to validate the physical implemen-tation of a HESS for urban commercial vehicles. A 30 kW experimental platform is built [23] to quantify the eco-nomic benefits of a semi-active HESS in reducing battery capacity fade costs under standard driving cycles. Song et al. [24] pointed out that in 2028, the feasibility of HESS may be compromised due to decreased battery price. Despite these e ff orts in sizing, onboard energy man-agement, and techno-economic analysis, it is still hard to answer the question: When are supercapacitors prac-tically feasible in electric vehicles? (1) existing siz-ing studies overlook the physical mass-volume packag-ing penalties and feasible serial-parallel configurations, i.e., energy management assessments under specific serial-parallel candidates are impracticable; (2) the design of energy management strategies and life-cycle economic analyses based on a single vehicle type are often one-sided, failing to account for the diverse spatial constraints and load capacity margins across di ff erent vehicle types; (3) feasibility evaluations of HESS lack a comprehensive quantification of mass, volume, battery lifespan, additional SC costs, total cost of ownership (TCO), future energy storage prices, and the influence of emerging solid-state batteries. 

1.3. This study 

The motivation, research gap, and the proposed multi-dimensional techno-economic evaluation for HESS fea-sibility of this study are provided in Fig. 1. Firstly, parameters of four representative electric vehicles, i.e., Sedan /SUV /Bus /Truck, are provided. Considering the 2voltage range, mass, and volume constraints of four dif-ferent battery packs, feasible supercapacitor serial-parallel number candidates are determined. Secondly, for each spe-cific feasible candidate, the supercapacitor pack mass and vehicle mass are updated. For di ff erent vehicles, dedi-cated standard driving cycles are selected for o ffl ine dy-namic programming-based power allocation optimization to minimize battery degradation and traction electricity costs. Thirdly, by analyzing the battery aging Pareto front and accounting for supercapacitor placement /initial cost, the optimal supercapacitor sizing for the four vehicles is selected. A state-of-the-art energy management strategy based on Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradi-ent (TD3) and behavioral cloning (BC) is implemented to quantify the online performance. Finally, the battery life extension, energy storage price, TCO, economy, mass ef-fect, placement (volume), and future solid-state battery era are comprehensively analyzed. The feasibility of HESS for di ff erent vehicle types is thoroughly concluded. The contributions of this study are listed below: (1) A cross-vehicle sizing method is established to explicitly quantify the physical mass-volume packaging penalties and determine the feasible SC candidates across di ff erent vehicle types. (2) An expert-guided artificial intelligence energy man-agement strategy is integrated to ensure a fair life-economy assessment for di ff erent vehicle types. (3) A comprehensive techno-economic feasibility ma-trix is constructed so that more general recommendations of HESS can be concluded. This study is organized as follows. In Section 2, pre-liminaries of HESS system models and detailed vehicle parameters are presented. The o ffl ine DP sizing and on-line DRL-BC energy management methods are proposed in Section 3. Section 4 provides the comprehensive fea-sibility analysis and HESS application recommendations. Followed by the conclusions in Section 5. 

2. Preliminaries 

The high-voltage battery pack is the only energy source of electric vehicles. Typically, the battery pack is con-nected to the traction motor through a DC /AC inverter; the motor is responsible for electrical-mechanical energy con-version. Between the motor and the wheels /di ff erentials, there is one single-speed reduction gearbox (another AMT for a truck) for increasing torque and reducing rotational speed, as shown in Fig. 2. To introduce the supercapacitor pack as the power bu ff er device, the most practical solution is the semi-active topol-ogy shown in Fig. 2. The supercapacitor pack has a rel-atively low voltage, which can be boosted to the battery pack voltage through a bidirectional DC /DC converter.

3.4. Optimal sizing 

DP results for the four di ff erent electric vehicles on bat-tery capacity loss are provided in Fig. 6. Sedan and SUV are tested under the CLTC driving cycle; the Bus is tested under the CHTC-B driving cycle; and the Truck is tested under the CHTC-D, Uphill, and Downhill driving cycles. These dedicated driving cycles are shown in Fig. 7. For the Bus and Truck under CLTC-D, half loads of 2450kg and 9000kg are considered. Specifically, for the Truck, transportation tasks at mine shafts or mines are also con-sidered, i.e., Uphill and Downhill cycles [13, 31] shown in Fig. 7 (d). In Fig. 6 (a), it is clear that the 1p candidates and 2p candidates are separated. And the separated candidates can be smoothly connected by the infeasible ones. For an ini-tial SoC sc of 0.95, the more supercapacitor cells, the lower battery capacity loss due to enough stored energy. When the initial SoC sc is 0.75, the same as the final value, there is a minimum battery degradation point between 160 and 7180 cells, which means more supercapacitor cells can be a burden; the battery pack must output more energy to maintain SoC sc . For an initial SoC sc of 0.50, the mini-mum battery degradation point comes earlier. By consid-ering the three di ff erent initial SoC sc values, the optimal supercapacitor sizing is 109s 1p, marked in red. The cur-rent supercapacitor price is still high, 2000-6000USD /kWh from EIT InnoEnergy [32]. In this study, an average of 4000USD /kWh, i.e., 28000CNY /kWh, is adopted. Thus, the optimal supercapacitor pack will cost about 9156 CNY. Similarly, the SUV and Bus also have three separated but continuous battery capacity loss Pareto fronts, see Fig. 6 (b) and Fig. 6 (c). And the optimal sizing results are both selected as the maximum 1p, 244s 1p (20496CNY) and 166s 1p (13944CNY), respectively. Note that the SUV uses the latest kilovolt battery platform (about 950 V); the supercapacitor pack must also be high-voltage. Even when the initial SoC sc is above 0.75, it still results in a low bat-tery capacity loss. Also, the optimal sizing of the Bus is significantly cheaper than that of the SUV , since urban bus driving cycles involve frequent starts and stops and a relatively low top velocity. Regarding the Truck, it can be seen that there are three minimum battery degradation zones (at the 0.75 curves) for CHTC-D, Uphill, and Downhill cycles in Fig. 6 (d-f), from 500 to 1500 cells. For an initial SoC sc of 0.95, the battery capacity loss curves are all monotonically decreas-ing under the three driving cycles. For an initial SoC sc 

of 0.5, the minimum battery degradation zones come ear-lier. Thus, the optimal sizing is selected as 200s 5p (84000CNY) to suit di ff erent driving cycles. Note that the battery capacity loss under Uphill is significantly higher than that under CHTC-D and Downhill, since the full-load uphill requires much more energy. From Fig. 6 (a-f), it can also be concluded that the op-timal battery capacity loss has nothing to do with the su-percapacitor serial-parallel configuration, i.e., as long as the cell total number is the same, di ff erent serial-parallel configurations will result in the same battery degradation (many rhombuses overlap). Thus, high-voltage configura-tion with less parallel number is recommended for lower line power loss. For the Bus under CHTC-B, the DRL-BC strategy sig-nificantly reduces the total operation cost from 197.5 to 82.6CNY /100km compared to the battery-only case, pri-marily driven by a reduction in degradation cost, from 166.5 to 51.6CNY /100km. For the Sedan and SUV, the to-tal operation costs are 28.3 and 29.8CNY /100km with the DRL-BC strategy, tracking closely with the DP results of 23.8 and 28.5CNY /100km, respectively. The near-optimal performance of the proposed energy management strategy has then been proved. However, for the Truck under the continuous heavy-duty Uphill cycle, the total operation cost decreases only slightly from 541.8 (battery-only) to 539.2CNY /100km (DRL-BC). The battery degradation cost remains high at 337.5CNY /100km, as the limited energy capacity of the supercapacitor pack is insu ffi cient for sustained discharge. While the operation cost comparisons validate the e ff ec-tiveness of the energy management strategy, commercial-izing HESS requires a comprehensive techno-economic assessment. The initial cost of the supercapacitor and the severe spatial placement penalties must be weighed against these cost savings, which necessitate the multi-dimensional feasibility framework discussed below. 

4.2. Feasibility analysis of the hybrid energy storage sys-tem 

To present a comprehensive techno-economic assess-ment, a five-dimensional feasibility framework is estab-lished. Five crucial dimensions are mapped into a normal-ized feasibility score ranging from 0 to 100, where 100 consistently represents the most favorable engineering or economic condition. The mapping boundary conditions are defined as follows: 

(1) Battery Life Extension : A positive metric normal-ized from 0% (score 0) to 80% (score 100), reflecting the percentage of battery degradation reduction by the DRL-BC energy management strategy. 

(2) TCO Reduction : A positive metric normalized from 0% (score 0) to 70% (score 100), denoting the overall op-eration cost savings over the entire vehicle lifespan. 

(3) SC Cost E ffi ciency : An inverse metric mapping the SC /battery initial investment ratio, normalized from 40% (score 0) to 0% (score 100). A score approaching 100 indicates that the SC’s initial investment is economically negligible compared to the battery pack. 

(4) Mass E ffi ciency : An inverse metric mapping the SC /battery mass ratio, ranging from 20% (score 0) to 0% (score 100). A score approaching 0 denotes negligible ad-ditional mass. 

(5) Placement Feasibility : An inverse metric mapping the SC /battery volume ratio, normalized from 45% (score 0) to 0% (score 100). A score approaching 0 indicates se-vere spatial placement constraints. The study concludes that the commercial and engineer-ing feasibility of HESS is not solely determined by the ve-hicle category (e.g., passenger versus heavy-duty truck), but is also inherently constrained by its operating load-frequency profile. High-frequency transient loads make HESS feasible, whereas relatively continuous load profiles make it impractical. It can be concluded that, for passenger vehicles, the HESS is expected in the future, i.e., the supercapacitor pack can be an optional premium configuration. For city buses, HESS is definitely recommended. For heavy-duty trucks, HESS deployment is not recommended in mining operations like mine shafts or mines, whereas it is highly feasible for urban and port transportation. 

5. Conclusion 

This study establishes a multi-dimensional techno-economic evaluation framework to systematically assess the feasibility for HESS in electric vehicles. By integrat-ing cross-vehicle optimal sizing, mass-volume constraints, online energy management, and life-cycle feasibility ma-trices, the main conclusions are drawn as follows: The life-cycle economic benefits of supercapacitors are highly sensitive to physical mass-volume penalties. Cur-rently, city buses are the most promising vehicle type for HESS applications, capable of mitigating battery degrada-tion at very low additional cost. Conversely, standard pas-senger vehicles and heavy-duty trucks for urban /port sce-narios remain constrained by spatial or heavy-load penal-ties; their HESS adoption will become feasible given a sig-nificant future reduction in supercapacitor prices. Beyond vehicle types, the techno-economic feasibility of HESS is fundamentally governed by the frequency char-acteristics of the load power. Vehicles with highly tran-sient, high-frequency power demands maximize the ben-efits of HESS, whereas relatively continuous load profiles render HESS economically unjustifiable. The economic benefits of HESS will diminish as the price collapse and technological evolution of batteries un-fold. Looking toward the 2030 + era, extending battery lifespan and energy density will further compress the need for onboard supercapacitors. Nevertheless, the high initial costs of solid-state batteries significantly amplify the asset protection leverage of degradation mitigation. By protect-ing the high-value batteries, the HESS remains an interest-ing solution for future electric vehicles. Several future directions are still interesting: (1) Fu-ture studies could integrate thermal safety dynamics into the economic evaluation. Supercapacitors inherently re-duce peak currents, which contributes to reducing battery heat generation. This capability not only relieves the op-erational load on the vehicle thermal management system but also o ff ers specific functional advantages in sub-zero climates, where supercapacitors can mitigate cold-weather battery constraints, e.g., lithium plating and power fade. (2) Guided by the load-frequency dependence, future re-search could explore the applicability of HESS in extreme-duty and o ff -road sectors. Vehicles such as electric racing vehicles, heavy construction machinery, or tracked vehi-cles are characterized by highly transient, stochastic power demands resulting from rapid accelerations, variable oper-ational resistances, or skid-steering mechanisms. In these specialized applications, the frequent load fluctuations can amplify the asset protection leverage of supercapacitors, presenting a practical scenario for future HESS integration.
